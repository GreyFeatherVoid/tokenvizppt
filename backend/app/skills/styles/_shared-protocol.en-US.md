## Design contract requirements

Derive a concrete deck-level visual contract from this style skill. The contract must specify:

- `theme`: the visual mood and design direction, not the deck topic.
- `background`: the canvas background behavior, including whether it is flat, gradient, paper-like, dark, or atmospheric.
- `palette`: 3-6 stable colors with clear roles for background, ink, accent, muted text, surface, and line.
- `typography`: title/body pairing, weight, scale, and hierarchy rhythm.
- `layout_rules`: how pages should distribute title, main message, evidence, visual anchor, and page number.
- `visual_motifs`: reusable motifs such as rules, seals, cards, grids, panels, geometry, dividers, or metric chips.

## Page semantics

Choose the layout from the slide's semantic role, not from a fixed template:

- Cover or divider: title can be the visual anchor; use one memorable motif and very little body text.
- Data-focus: make the metric, comparison, chart-like structure, or evidence block the visual center.
- Comparison: use clear left/right, before/after, matrix, or paired-card structures.
- Timeline or process: use a directional structure with stages, connectors, and explicit sequence.
- Concept: use a central model, metaphor, diagram, or asymmetric editorial layout.
- Summary: use synthesis cards, a strong closing statement, or next-step structure.
- Image-focus: give the image intentional space and adjust text around it.

## Composition rules

- Vary composition across slides while keeping the same visual language.
- Do not repeat the same title position, same card grid, or same two-column layout on every page.
- Keep one dominant focal area per slide.
- Use short text modules; reduce copy before shrinking type.
- Use whitespace as an active design element.
- Use shapes for visual rhythm; do not create arrows, icons, rules, or ornaments as text.
- Leave enough breathing room for PowerPoint export; browser and PPT font metrics differ.

## Data and evidence rules

- When points contain numbers, trends, rankings, proportions, or comparisons, turn them into metric cards, compact tables, bar-like structures, slope comparisons, or labeled evidence blocks.
- Never invent precise data, dates, institutions, or rankings that are not in the user brief or source material.
- Prefer one clear takeaway over many small labels.
- Keep labels readable and aligned; avoid tiny bottom legends.

## Image rules

- Uploaded or generated images must be placed for a semantic reason, not as decoration.
- If an image contains data, screenshot content, or document text, include a concise textual takeaway near it.
- If an image is decorative or atmospheric, it should support the slide mood without competing with the headline.
- Do not place images on top of text or as low-value filler.

## Hard avoid

- Avoid generic SaaS purple gradients unless the style explicitly calls for them.
- Avoid emoji, sticker-like decoration, random icons, dense paragraphs, and clutter.
- Avoid overlapping text boxes, tiny captions, invisible elements, and bottom-heavy layouts.
- Avoid mixing incompatible visual systems inside one deck.
